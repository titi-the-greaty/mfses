TKR Ticker Stock symbol & company name ▾
What it is
A ticker symbol is the short abbreviation used to uniquely identify a publicly traded company on a stock exchange. For example, AAPL = Apple Inc., MSFT = Microsoft Corp. These are assigned by the exchange (NYSE or NASDAQ) when a company goes public via an IPO.
On SEESAW
The ticker column shows both the symbol and full company name. Clicking a ticker opens the detailed stock view with all sub-metrics.

PRC Price Current share price ▾
What it is
The current market price of one share of stock. This is the last traded price on the exchange and fluctuates throughout the trading day based on supply and demand. It reflects what buyers are willing to pay and what sellers are willing to accept.
Why it matters
Price alone doesn't tell you if a stock is "cheap" or "expensive" — a $500 stock can be undervalued while a $10 stock can be overvalued. That's why SEESAW pairs price with valuation metrics like P/E, Graham Number, and Upside %.

CHG% Daily Change % Today's price movement ▾
What it is
The percentage change in stock price from yesterday's close to the current price. A positive number (green) means the stock is up today; negative (red) means it's down.
Formula
Chg% = ((Current Price − Previous Close) / Previous Close) × 100

STATE State HOT / WARM / COLD / FROZEN ▾
What it is
A SEESAW-proprietary composite health rating that distills all 6 factor scores into one intuitive label: HOT (strong across the board), WARM (mostly positive), COLD (mixed or weak), FROZEN (poor fundamentals or outlook).
How it works
The state is derived from the Total Score — the sum of all six factor scores (Moat, Growth, Balance, Value, Sentiment, Dividend). Score thresholds determine which state label a stock receives. Higher total scores → hotter state.

MOAT Moat Score Competitive advantage strength ▾
What it is
Inspired by Warren Buffett's concept of an economic moat — a company's ability to maintain competitive advantages over rivals and protect long-term profits. Think of it as a castle's moat: the wider it is, the harder it is for competitors to attack.
What feeds the score
Return on Equity (ROE) — how efficiently a company uses shareholder money. Return on Invested Capital (ROIC) — profitability relative to total capital. Profit margins — how much of each dollar of revenue becomes profit. Market dominance — brand strength and market share.

GRW Growth Score Revenue & earnings growth quality ▾
What it is
Measures how fast a company is increasing its revenue and earnings over time. High-growth companies are expanding rapidly; low-growth or negative-growth companies are stagnating or shrinking.
What feeds the score
Revenue growth rate (year-over-year), EPS growth (earnings per share trajectory), forward estimates (what analysts project), and consistency of growth across multiple quarters.

BAL Balance Score Balance sheet health ▾
What it is
Evaluates a company's financial health and stability. A strong balance sheet means the company can weather downturns, invest in growth, and meet its obligations. A weak balance sheet means the company is overleveraged or cash-strapped.
What feeds the score
Debt-to-Equity ratio — how much debt vs. shareholder equity. Current Ratio — can it pay short-term bills? Interest Coverage — can it service its debt? Free Cash Flow — cash left after all expenses and investments.

VAL Value Score Valuation attractiveness ▾
What it is
Determines whether a stock is undervalued, fairly valued, or overvalued relative to its fundamentals. Uses multiple valuation models to triangulate a fair price — if the stock trades significantly below that price, there may be an opportunity.
What feeds the score
P/E Ratio — price relative to earnings. P/B Ratio — price relative to book value. PEG Ratio — P/E adjusted for growth. Graham Number — Ben Graham's intrinsic value formula. DCF — discounted cash flow model. Bond-adjusted valuation — comparing equity returns to bond yields.

TOT Total Score Sum of all 6 factor scores ▾
What it is
The aggregate of all six factor scores (Moat + Growth + Balance + Value + Sentiment + Dividend). This single number gives you a holistic view of a stock's quality across every dimension SEESAW tracks.
Formula

S/M/L Short / Mid / Long Term Time-horizon outlook scores ▾
What they are
Three separate scores that weight the 6 factors differently depending on time horizon. Short-term emphasizes momentum, sentiment, and technicals. Mid-term balances growth with valuation. Long-term prioritizes moat, balance sheet strength, and dividend sustainability.
Why it matters
A stock can be great for long-term holders but terrible for short-term traders, or vice versa. These scores help you match stocks to your investment timeline.
UPS Upside % Gap to estimated intrinsic value ▾
What it is
The percentage difference between the current market price and SEESAW's estimated intrinsic value (using Graham/DCF models). A positive upside means the stock is potentially undervalued; negative means potentially overvalued.
Formula
Upside% = ((Intrinsic Value − Current Price) / Current Price) × 100

Flags are warning and opportunity signals. Triple Crowns are rare and indicate all-around quality. Value Traps warn you not to be fooled by a low P/E. Expensive Growth reminds you that great companies can still be bad investments if you overpay.

Dividend Deep Dive

6 metrics — income investing essentials
▾
EPSEarnings Per Share (EPS)Profit per share of stock▾
What it is
The company's net profit divided by the number of outstanding shares. EPS tells you how much money a company earns for each share you own. It's the foundation of most valuation metrics like P/E ratio.
Formula
EPS = Net Income / Shares Outstanding

PAYPayout Ratio% of earnings paid as dividends▾
What it is
The percentage of a company's earnings that go toward paying dividends. A 40% payout means the company pays out 40 cents of every dollar earned and keeps 60 cents for reinvestment. Too high (>80%) can signal the dividend is unsustainable.
Formula
Payout Ratio = (Dividends per Share / EPS) × 100

5YG5-Year Dividend GrowthAverage annual dividend increase▾
What it is
The compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of a company's dividend over the last 5 years. A company paying $1.00 five years ago and $1.50 today has roughly a 8.4% 5-year growth rate. Consistent growers are more reliable income sources.

CONConsecutive YearsStreak of annual dividend increases▾
What it is
How many years in a row a company has raised its dividend. 25+ years = Dividend Aristocrat. 50+ years = Dividend King. This streak is a powerful signal of management's commitment to returning cash to shareholders.

EXDEx-Dividend DateLast day to buy for the next payout▾
What it is
The cutoff date — you must own the stock before this date to receive the upcoming dividend payment. If you buy on or after the ex-dividend date, the seller gets the dividend, not you.

GRMGraham NumberBen Graham's intrinsic value formula▾
What it is
A valuation formula created by Benjamin Graham (Warren Buffett's mentor and the father of value investing). It calculates the maximum price a defensive investor should pay for a stock based on its earnings and book value.
Formula
Graham Number = √(22.5 × EPS × Book Value per Share)
Why it matters
If a stock's price is below its Graham Number, it may be undervalued by conservative standards. SEESAW uses a bond-adjusted version that accounts for current interest rates, making it more relevant to today's market.

Technical Indicators

8 indicators — price action & momentum
▾
SMA20SMA 2020-day Simple Moving Average▾
What it is
The average closing price over the last 20 trading days. It smooths out daily noise to show the short-term trend. If the price is above the SMA20, the stock is in a short-term uptrend; below it means a downtrend.
Formula
SMA20 = (Sum of last 20 closing prices) / 20

SMA50SMA 5050-day Simple Moving Average▾
What it is
The average closing price over 50 trading days. A key medium-term trend indicator. The golden cross (SMA50 crosses above SMA200) and death cross (SMA50 crosses below SMA200) are two of the most watched signals in technical analysis.
Learn More
wiki Wikipedia: MA Crossovers
EMA12EMA 1212-day Exponential Moving Average▾
What it is
Like SMA but gives more weight to recent prices, making it more responsive to new information. The 12-day EMA is the "fast line" — it reacts quickly to price changes and is a key component of MACD.
SMA vs EMA
SMA treats all days equally. EMA emphasizes recent days more. EMA reacts faster to new trends but can give more false signals. Most traders use both.

wiki Wikipedia: Exponential MA
EMA26EMA 2626-day Exponential Moving Average▾
What it is
The "slow line" counterpart to EMA12. The difference between EMA12 and EMA26 is what creates the MACD indicator. When EMA12 crosses above EMA26, it signals bullish momentum; below signals bearish.
BBBollinger BandsVolatility bands around price▾
What it is
Three lines plotted around the price: a 20-day SMA in the middle, with an upper band (+2 standard deviations) and lower band (-2 standard deviations). When the bands squeeze tight, a big move is coming. When price touches the upper band, the stock may be overbought; lower band, oversold.
Formula
Upper Band = SMA20 + (2 × StdDev) Middle Band = SMA20 Lower Band = SMA20 − (2 × StdDev)

RSIRSI (Relative Strength Index)Momentum oscillator (0–100)▾
What it is
A momentum indicator that measures the speed and magnitude of recent price changes on a scale of 0 to 100. Above 70 = potentially overbought (due for a pullback). Below 30 = potentially oversold (due for a bounce). It's one of the most widely used technical indicators.
Formula
RSI = 100 − (100 / (1 + RS)) RS = Average Gain over 14 periods / Average Loss over 14 periods

MACDMACDTrend & momentum indicator▾
What it is
One of the most popular technical indicators. It shows the relationship between two EMAs (12-day and 26-day). When the MACD line crosses above the signal line, it's a bullish signal; below is bearish. The histogram shows the strength of the momentum.
Formula
MACD Line = EMA12 − EMA26 Signal Line = 9-day EMA of MACD Line Histogram = MACD Line − Signal Line

What it is
The average price a stock has traded at throughout the day, weighted by volume. It's the benchmark institutions use to judge execution quality. If you bought below VWAP, you got a "good fill." Price above VWAP = bullish intraday; below = bearish.

CFCash FlowCash generation & usage▾
What it is
Tracks the actual cash moving in and out of the business. Unlike earnings (which can be manipulated with accounting tricks), cash flow shows real money. Free Cash Flow (FCF) — cash after all expenses and investments — is what's available for dividends, buybacks, and debt repayment.

GROGrowth MetricsRevenue & earnings trajectory▾
What it is
Measures the rate of increase in a company's revenue and earnings over time. Includes year-over-year growth, quarter-over-quarter growth, and multi-year compound growth rates. Consistently high growth is the hallmark of great businesses.

Gross Margin — revenue minus cost of goods, as a %. How much profit before overhead. Operating Margin — profit after all operating expenses. Net Margin — the final bottom-line percentage after everything (taxes, interest, etc.). Higher margins = more profitable business model.

SEESAW Scoring System

5 concepts — how scores are built
▾
6F6-Factor ScoringThe core methodology▾
How it works
Every stock is scored across six fundamental dimensions: Moat (competitive advantage), Growth (earnings trajectory), Balance (financial health), Value (price vs. worth), Sentiment (market mood), and Dividend (income quality). Each factor is scored independently, then combined into the Total Score.
Why 6 factors?
No single metric tells the full story. A cheap stock (high Value) might be cheap for a reason (low Moat). A fast grower (high Growth) might be running on debt (low Balance). The 6-factor system forces a 360° view of every company.
TMHTime Horizon ScoringShort / Mid / Long weighting▾
How it works
The same 6 factors are re-weighted for different investment timelines. Short-term overweights Sentiment and technicals. Mid-term balances Growth and Value. Long-term overweights Moat, Balance, and Dividend sustainability. This lets you match stocks to your holding period.
STAState ClassificationHow HOT/WARM/COLD/FROZEN is assigned▾
How it works
The Total Score is mapped to one of four states using threshold ranges. HOT = top-tier total scores. WARM = above-average. COLD = below-average. FROZEN = weakest scores. The thresholds are calibrated across the full stock universe so each state represents a meaningful quality tier.
FLGFlag RulesWhen special flags are triggered▾
How they work
Flags are conditional labels triggered by specific score patterns: Triple Crown — all three time horizons (Short, Mid, Long) score above a high threshold. Value Trap — high Value score but low Moat and/or declining Growth (looks cheap, but deteriorating). Expensive Growth — high Growth score but low Value score (great business, but overpriced).
UPCUpside CalculationHow intrinsic value is estimated▾
How it works
SEESAW combines multiple valuation models to estimate a stock's fair intrinsic value, then compares it to the market price. Models include the Graham Number (conservative, earnings + book value), DCF (forward-looking cash flow projection), and a bond-adjusted version that factors in current interest rates to make valuations more relevant.
